Diuretic Therapy Decision Support

Stepped assessment of congestion and diuretic titration — based on the 2019 HFA-ESC Position Statement (Mullens et al., Eur J Heart Fail 2019;21:137–155)

Step 1 — Initial Loop Diuretic Dose

Confirm congestion is due to volume overload before initiating. Have patient empty bladder and start urine collection immediately after first dose.

Patient Profile

Pre-Dose Checklist

1. Empty bladder (consider bladder scan) before first dose.
2. Start urine collection immediately after first dose.
3. Begin parallel monitoring: HR, rhythm, RR, SpO₂, signs of hypoperfusion, baseline weight, BP.
4. Consider early: continue GDMT, early MRA if not contraindicated, salt/water restriction, IV K⁺/Mg²⁺ if low.

Step 2 — Early Evaluation (First 6 Hours)

At 2 h: check spot urinary sodium. At 6 h: assess average hourly urine output over the period.

Step 3 — Day-2 Algorithm: 24-Hour Urine Output

If congestion persists at 24 h, evaluate total urine output and adjust accordingly.

24-Hour Urine Output

Combination Diuretic Therapy (Sequential Nephron Blockade)

Maximal loop diuretic dose reached with insufficient output. Add agents in sequence:
First line — Thiazide / thiazide-like diuretic
Metolazone 2.5–10 mg PO (give several hours before loop dose — slow absorption, peak up to 8 h)
or Chlorothiazide 500–1000 mg IV (onset ~30 min — give closer to loop dose)
or HCTZ 25–100 mg PO
Second line — Acetazolamide or amiloride
Acetazolamide 500 mg PO/IV — targets proximal nephron sodium avidity, reduces RAAS activation via macula densa effects.
Amiloride 5–10 mg PO.
Third line — SGLT2 inhibitor
Additional natriuretic agent targeting the proximal tubule (e.g. empagliflozin/dapagliflozin) — consider if not already on GDMT-dose SGLT2i.
Bail-out

If congestion persists despite maximal combination therapy → consider ultrafiltration. CARRESS-HF showed stepped pharmacologic care achieves equal decongestion to UF with fewer serious adverse events — UF is not first-line.

Chronic Ambulatory Dosing Principles

• Loop diuretics: Class I across full LVEF spectrum to prevent congestion — limited mortality/morbidity evidence in chronic HF.

• Use lowest effective dose; re-assess need after CRT or sacubitril/valsartan initiation.

• Avoid chronic ambulatory thiazide (sequential nephron blockade) where possible — risk of severe, undetected electrolyte disturbance outpatient.

• Salt intake: avoid >6 g NaCl/day (2.4 g Na⁺); avoid excessive fluid intake. Routine severe restriction (<2 g/day) not recommended.

• If furosemide pre-admission, consider switching to bumetanide or torsemide for more predictable absorption.

Diuretic Resistance — Identification & Management

Prevalence 20–50% of hospitalised HF patients. Based on ADVOR, CLOROTIC, EMPULSE, DAPA-RESIST, EVEREST, ATHENA-HF, and Lima et al. NMA 2026.

Identify Diuretic Resistance — Any of the Following

• Persistent congestion despite escalating loop diuretic doses
• Spot urine Na⁺ < 50–65 mEq/L at 6 hours post-dose
• Urine output < 100 mL/h at 6 hours post-dose
• Weight loss < 1 kg/day on maximum-dose IV loop diuretics

Identify & Treat Underlying Cause

Prerenal / Haemodynamic
• Low cardiac output → ↓ renal perfusion
• Venous congestion → ↑ renal venous pressure
• Elevated intra-abdominal pressure
Treatment: Inotropes · Vasodilators · Paracentesis
Intrarenal / Tubular
• DCT hypertrophy with compensatory Na⁺ reabsorption
• RAAS/SNS neurohormonal activation
• Post-diuretic sodium retention & braking
Treatment: Sequential nephron blockade · RAASi
Pharmacokinetic
• Gut oedema delays oral absorption (furosemide bioavailability 10–90%)
• Hypoalbuminaemia ↓ tubular secretion
• Uremic toxins compete with OAT transporters
Treatment: Switch to IV · Torsemide/bumetanide
Trigger for add-on therapy

Urine output < 3–4 L/24 h on maximum loop diuretic dose → add sequential nephron blockade (below).

Add-On Agent Selection

SGLT2 Inhibitors — Initiate Early (First-Line)
Agents & dose: Empagliflozin 10–25 mg OD · Dapagliflozin 10 mg OD
Mechanism: Proximal tubule SGLT2 + NHE3 inhibition → natriuresis without RAAS activation
Key trials: EMPAG-HF +25% cumulative UO over 5 days · EMPULSE significant benefit at 90 days · DAPA-RESIST comparable to metolazone, fewer adverse events · NMA 2026: dapagliflozin OR 0.15 (0.03–0.73), empagliflozin OR 0.19 (0.04–0.94) for HF hospitalisation
Contraindications: eGFR < 20 mL/min/1.73m² · Type 1 diabetes (DKA risk)
Preferred phenotype: All phenotypes — especially electrolyte derangement or hyponatraemia risk
▸ No electrolyte monitoring▸ Favourable AKI signal▸ GDMT benefit
Thiazide / Thiazide-Like — Distal Nephron Blockade
Agents & dose: Metolazone 2.5–10 mg PO (t½ 12–24h) · Chlorothiazide 500 mg IV (onset 30 min) · HCTZ 25–100 mg PO (eGFR-adjusted)
Mechanism: DCT NCC cotransporter blockade → blocks compensatory Na⁺ reabsorption at hypertrophied DCT cells
Key trials: CLOROTIC weight −2.3 vs −1.5 kg at 72h (p=0.002) · NMA 2026 largest standardised weight loss SMD 1.33 (0.49–2.18) · 3T trial comparable to tolvaptan for 48h weight loss
Cautions: Highest AKI signal in NMA — HCTZ OR 4.14 (2.03–8.42); hypokalaemia, hyponatraemia, hyperuricaemia
Preferred phenotype: Refractory oedema; hyperkalaemia from RAASi
⚠ Monitor electrolytes daily⚠ Monitor creatinineEffective in CKD
Acetazolamide (CA Inhibitor) — Proximal Nephron Blockade
Dose: 500 mg IV OD (ADVOR regimen) — short-term use only
Mechanism: Inhibits carbonic anhydrase II & IV in proximal tubule → ↑ NaHCO₃ excretion; overcomes neurohormone-driven proximal Na⁺ retention; neutral neurohormonal activation
Key trials: ADVOR decongestion at 72h 42.2% vs 30.5% (RR 1.46, p<0.001), shorter LOS · Greater benefit when HCO₃⁻ ≥ 27 mmol/L · Effect amplified with eGFR ≤ 40 mL/min
Cautions: Avoid long-term use (metabolic acidosis risk); monitor HCO₃⁻ and K⁺
Preferred phenotype: Metabolic alkalosis (HCO₃⁻ ≥ 27 mmol/L); proximal tubule resistance suspected; when thiazide electrolyte risk unacceptable
Monitor HCO₃⁻ & K⁺⚠ Avoid long-term useNo mortality signal
Tolvaptan (V2 Antagonist) — Aquaresis
Dose: 15–30 mg PO OD (short-term only)
Mechanism: V2 receptor blockade → free water excretion (aquaresis) without natriuresis
Key trials: NMA 2026 largest UO gain MD +1,840 mL (95% CI 441–3,240) vs low-dose furosemide · EVEREST improved short-term symptoms, neutral long-term mortality · Corrects hyponatraemia significantly
Cautions: Risk of liver toxicity — short-term use only
Preferred phenotype: Hyponatraemia (Na⁺ < 130); water-predominant congestion
Corrects hyponatraemia⚠ Liver toxicity risk
Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists
Agents & dose: Spironolactone 25–50 mg · Eplerenone · Finerenone
Mechanism: ENaC inhibition (indirect via mineralocorticoid receptor) → K⁺-sparing natriuresis
Key trials: ATHENA-HF — high-dose spironolactone 100 mg did NOT improve NT-proBNP or UO acutely · RALES — 30% mortality reduction in chronic HFrEF
Cautions: Contraindicated if K⁺ > 5.5 mEq/L; monitor K⁺ and creatinine closely
Preferred phenotype: Hypokalaemia; RAAS activation; HFrEF GDMT initiation
⚠ K⁺ > 5.5 = contraindicatedHFrEF mortality benefit

Phenotype-Guided Agent Selection

Monitoring During Decongestion

🔴 High-Risk Signals — Escalate

Creatinine rise > 0.5 mg/dL without decongestion · K⁺ < 3.0 or > 5.5 mEq/L · Systolic BP < 90 mmHg · Na⁺ < 125 mEq/L · Urine output < 1 mL/kg/h despite max therapy · Rising lactate (low cardiac output)

🟡 Routine Monitoring

Daily weight and fluid balance · Spot urine Na⁺ at 6h post-dose · Urea & electrolytes daily (twice daily if combination therapy) · HCO₃⁻ monitoring if acetazolamide · Target UO ≥ 3–4 L/day · Congestion score (oedema, JVP, ascites)

🟢 Reassuring Decongestion Signs

Weight loss ≥ 1–2 kg/day · Spot urine Na⁺ ≥ 50–70 mEq/L · Improving dyspnoea score · JVP < 8 cm H₂O · Resolving peripheral oedema & orthopnoea · Declining NT-proBNP/BNP

Refractory to All Pharmacological Strategies

Ultrafiltration
Reserve for truly refractory cases after all pharmacological options exhausted. CARRESS-HF: pharmacological therapy superior to UF for renal preservation. Contraindications: hypotension, severe infection, haemodynamic instability.
Haemodynamic Support
Inotropes if low cardiac output (MAP < 65, cool extremities, rising lactate) to restore renal perfusion and re-establish diuretic response. ROSE-AHF: low-dose dopamine showed no benefit in unselected HF with renal dysfunction.
Advanced HF Referral
LVAD candidacy assessment · Heart transplant evaluation · Implantable PA pressure monitoring (CHAMPION: ↓ HF hospitalisations at 6 months) · Palliative care discussion if appropriate.
